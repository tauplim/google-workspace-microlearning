It sounds like you are referring to the cognitive sequence of **Learn, Consolidate, and Compress** (assuming "leann" was a quick typo for "learn").

While it might not be trademarked as a single, formal methodology, it is one of the most accurate ways to describe how the human brain actually achieves mastery over a subject. Most people stop at the first step, which is why they forget what they study.

Here is a breakdown of how this three-part framework maps to cognitive psychology, and how you can use it to master any topic.

### **1. Learn (Acquisition & Encoding)**

This is the initial stage where you are exposed to new information. Your goal here is not memorization, but **understanding**. You are essentially taking raw data and encoding it into your short-term memory.

* **Focus is non-negotiable:** Learning requires high-quality attention. Multitasking destroys your brain's ability to encode new information.
* **Prime your brain:** Before diving into details, get a "big picture" view of the topic. Look at the table of contents or read a summary. It creates a scaffold for the details to hang onto.
* **Elaboration:** Don't just read passively. Ask yourself, "How does this connect to something I already know?" Tying new concepts to existing knowledge makes the initial memory trace much stronger.

### **2. Consolidate (Strengthening & Storage)**

Consolidation is the process of moving information from short-term memory into long-term storage. This is where the actual "wiring" of your brain changes, making the knowledge durable.

* **Active Recall:** Rereading notes is an illusion of competence. To consolidate, you must force your brain to retrieve the information from scratch. Flashcards, practice tests, or just writing down everything you remember on a blank sheet of paper are highly effective.
* **Spaced Repetition:** Review the material at increasing intervals (e.g., 1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 1 month). This interrupts the brain's natural "forgetting curve" and signals that the information is important enough to keep.
* **Sleep and Rest:** Biological consolidation physically occurs while you sleep, particularly during deep sleep and REM cycles. Without adequate rest, the neural connections you formed while studying will weaken and fade.

### **3. Compress (Mastery & Chunking)**

This is the "secret sauce" of top performers. Compression is about reducing the cognitive load. As you gain mastery, you group isolated pieces of information together into a single, intuitive concept (a "chunk").

* **The Feynman Technique:** Try to explain the concept in plain, simple English, as if you were teaching a 12-year-old. If you have to use complex jargon, you haven't compressed the knowledge enough.
* **Find the heuristics:** Look for underlying rules or patterns. Instead of memorizing 50 different instances of a rule, memorize the one core principle that governs all 50 instances.
* **Mental Models and Analogies:** Create metaphors. For example, understanding electricity as "water flowing through pipes" compresses complex physics equations into an intuitive visual image.

---

When you **learn**, you gather the puzzle pieces. When you **consolidate**, you lock the pieces together. When you **compress**, you stop seeing 1,000 individual pieces and start seeing the single, unified picture.
